How to grow and care for mint plants

Mint (Mentha spicata) is a fragrant, low-growing herb that spreads easily and is simple to cultivate. In fact, it can grow so vigorously that it may take over garden areas—so it's best to keep mintconfined to its own pot, garden bed, or container.

Mint thrives in spots ranging from full sun to partial shade, provided the soil stays moist. The sunnier the location, the more water your mint will need. It doesn't tolerate extended dry periods, so ensure it's in a place where it can be watered regularly, especially during hot, dry months.

There are many types of mint to choose from, including garden mint, spearmint, peppermint, chocolate mint, apple mint, and basil mint—each offering distinct flavours and aromas matching their names.

Top 5 steps to growing mint plants

  • Choose a sunny to shaded position with moist soil for planting mint
  • Mint can be propagated easily from seeds, stem cuttings, or starter plants
  • To prevent it from spreading uncontrollably, grow mint in a dedicated pot, bed, or planter
  • Pick the leaves as needed—they can be enjoyed fresh or preserved by drying
  • In colder climates, mint may die back in winter—cut it to the ground and apply a layer of Scotts Osmocote; it will regrow as temperatures rise

Planting Mint in the Garden

When planting mint directly in your garden, choose a contained area or a separate garden bed, as mint spreads rapidly and can crowd out other plants.

To plant, take the mint out of its nursery pot and place it in the middle of a prepared hole in the soil. If the roots are tightly packed, gently loosen them before planting. Water the plant thoroughly after planting, and apply mulch around it to help keep the soil moist and to reduce weed growth.

Allow the mint to settle and grow new shoots before you begin harvesting. You can snip the leaves with sharp scissors whenever needed. The young tips are especially soft and flavourful, but all\ leaves are usable.

To dry the leaves, use a dehydrator or hang bunches upside down in a dry, airy location until they become crisp. Store the dried leaves in an airtight container for later use.

How to Grow Mint from Seeds

Mint seeds can be planted at any time of the year, as long as it's not during extreme heat or cold. Fill seed trays or small pots with Scotts Osmocote® Seed & Cutting Mix, scatter the seeds on the surface, and cover them with a 1cm layer of potting mix.

Keep the mix damp—but not soaked—while waiting for the seeds to sprout, which usually takes 10 to 14 days.

Once the seedlings reach about 10cm in height, move them to their final growinglocation.

Add mulch around the mint plants to conserve moisture and prevent weeds from growing.

Growing Mint from Cuttings

Mint is very easy to propagate from cuttings. Snip a tip from an existing mint plant and place it in a glass of water on a sunny windowsill.

After the cutting develops roots, you can transplant it into a pot or directly into your garden bed, where it will continue to grow and thrive.
