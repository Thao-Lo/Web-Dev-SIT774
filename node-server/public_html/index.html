Introduction

Badminton is a widely enjoyed racket sport for both men and women, played in both singles and doubles formats. The objective is to use a racquet to hit a shuttlecock over a net so that it lands within the opponent’s court boundaries

History Of Badminton

Originating from the Indian game “Poona,” badminton began gaining popularity under British colonial influence in the 19th century.

The sport’s formal structure emerged with the first All England Championships in 1899, and later the English Badminton Association was established in 1893. In 1934, the International Badminton Federation—now the BWF—was founded.

Today, major international competitions include the Thomas Cup (from 1948) and Uber Cup (from 1956)

Rules of Badminton

A badminton match is played as the best of three games, with each game going up to 21 points using rally scoring, where a point is awarded on every serve. To win a game, a player must lead by at least two points, except when the score reaches 29–29, in which case the player who scores the 30th point wins.

When the server wins a rally, they continue serving from the opposite service court. During service, both players must remain inside their service boxes, and the shuttle must be struck below waist height without touching the ground.

Equipment

The badminton court is a rectangular playing surface divided by a net. It measures 13.4 meters long and is either 5.18 meters wide (singles) or 6.1 meters wide (doubles). The court includes marked zones for serving and rally play.

Racket

Standard racket size: 68 cm long with a 23 cm wide head. It has a thin shaft and a stringed oval head used to hit the shuttlecock.

A badminton racket is lightweight, usually made from carbon fiber or aluminum, designed to allow quick, controlled swings.

Badminton equipment is designed to enhance speed, control, and agility during play. Choosing the right gear improves performance and reduces the risk of injury.

Shuttlecock

Shuttlecock weight: between 4.74 and 5.50 g, typically with 16 feathers.

The shuttlecock, also known as a birdie, is a cone-shaped projectile with a rounded rubber or cork base and a skirt made of feathers or synthetic material.
